## Chapter VI: Upgrades and Accessories (November 2024)

At the first event with my own bike in Wuppertal I had the glorious idea of messing with tire pressure. So set to 1.7 cold and go. The result: a complete disaster. Everything back and ride. After 4 turns my right hand is almost numb. So the brake absolutely had to be converted. The friendly riders on the track are consistently helpful and give valuable tips for the conversion.

A Formula brake is recommended, as well as different footrests and narrow/light wheels.

![Brake and disc](/assets/images/pitbike-02.jpg)

Brake is ordered from Italy, brake disc (220mm) and footrests from China. I'll get the wheels later.

Unfortunately the brake caliper adapter didn't quite fit the larger brake disc. So I created a larger gap with M10 nuts as spacer sleeves and fastened it with proper screws. It still serves its purpose today.

![Footrest](/assets/images/pitbike-03.jpg)

With the footrests I decided to recycle the adjustable and folding rests from my old Honda. But instead of aluminum rests something made of plastic was needed. 3D printing myself is way too unstable. So I ordered plastic footrests from China, drilled them out, adapted them, shortened them and milled grooves for slip resistance. It also serves its purpose today.

At the beginning I still had hopes that I could ride here without tire warmers. This constant putting on and taking off is annoying. But what's even more annoying is the first laps without pre-warmed tires. I'm already exhausted when the tire starts to work. So I ordered some from Scooter-Attack and haven't ridden without them since.

## Chapter VIII: Next Upgrades to the Bike

Since I've already crashed a few times, I need to take care of steering limiters.

As always, see what you can buy. With one of the models, as an old miser I immediately recognized that I could 3D print it myself. So I bashed out 2-3 prototypes with my CAD program of choice and printed them. Recommendation: Use a material that is as flexible as possible. ABS or PLA or PETG are really only partially suitable. I printed it that way and I'm satisfied to this day.

## Chapter IX: New Tracks to Discover (March/April 2025)

In spring 2025 it became known that the operator of the go-kart track in Wuppertal was no longer continuing there and the hall would probably have a completely different use. Not cool when your home track is taken away.

Looking for alternatives, we explored in a group in Oberhausen. Unfortunately it can be summed up in one word: Unrideable.

Fortunately it's getting warmer again and the outdoor go-kart track at Spa-Francorchamps isn't too far away. One thing upfront: The track layout, the surface and the fun are unbeatable! Only with the framework conditions is there definitely room for improvement. My personal critique:

- The booking page of 2race.be was apparently programmed by a drunk uncle of a gross motor skill person. One of the worst information and booking systems I've ever seen.
- The driver area is not continuously asphalted. Consists mostly of gravel, dirt and mud. Without a carpet as a base, nothing works here.
- There are exactly 6 power outlets for the entire driver area. Of these, 5 work. On an average day there are definitely 150 to 200 riders. Accordingly, generators are constantly humming around you. Very unpleasant!
- There are usually 2 groups for pitbikers (fast/slow) and then 2 more for SuMo riders. On the track it's accordingly quite crowded and the homogeneity of the groups is suboptimal.

Each group has 15 minutes of driving time per session. So each group gets 6 turns spread throughout the day.

**Procedure:**

Book the days you want to ride on the booking page. There doesn't seem to be a quota.

On arrival at the track, it's best to secure a free outlet right away. To do this you have to get there early and find it first. A long extension cord is a must!

Then register at one of the terminals (screens on the wall) in the Race-Office/Bistro.

Then line up in the queue and pick up group stickers.

After that pay at the checkout (if you found an outlet, remember the number on it and have it unlocked, costs an extra €10) and receive your membership card. The membership card costs €5 per year and saves you registration at the terminal next time.

From now on everything as you know it from the race track: Be on time for the pre-start and have fun.

Although I've given Spa a lot of criticism, I've been there multiple times and will come back as often as possible. Deliciously awesome!

## Chapter X: Tires, Wheels and Carburetor (June 2025)

When I bought the bike, I had promised myself that the carburetor would only be changed when it starts acting up and the tires stay on until they're done. Since tire wear when riding outdoors is many times higher than on indoor tracks, it was time. The six-year-old Maxxis R1 (semi-slick) will be replaced with PMT slicks. I also picked up narrower wheels from Apex-Pitbike at a ridiculously cheap price on the occasion. Not the fancy ultra-light variant, just narrower. And because I'm getting yellow spots in my pants at the thought of being on a big race track in the rain on a pitbike, I got brave and picked up rain tires for the old wheels.

While the big boys were competing in the EWC 8-hour endurance race at Spa, I had booked myself in at the go-kart track and the rain tires came into use right away. Boy-oh-boy, we still have to practice that!

When it comes to PMT slicks and wheels, I unfortunately have to say that I don't feel any noticeable difference. My fastest lap time at Spa was also with the old tires and wheels. So if you want to try something different: The Maxxis R1 tires are sometimes available at half price and I can't say anything bad about them. Whether these differ in warm-up behavior compared to the PMT when you ride without a warmer, I also can't report.

Since a new/better carburetor has been on the wish list for a long time anyway, and I had some situations when switching from half to full throttle where I felt some jerking, I now installed a Nibbi PE26. Again: The original carburetor worked well in about 30 operating hours and the update was actually a nice-to-have.

Here too, hardly any noticeable differences with the Nibbi. Throttle response at load change slightly better. That's it. Instead, the included Nibbi rubber flange cracked after just a few months and had to be replaced.

## Chapter XI: The Country Needs New Tracks (June 2025)

The FLM brand (Polo) offers free riding on the grounds of Polo Jüchen with organizer Abenteuer-Motorrad. Here you build a track with tires and tape and you can really go for it. The grip is breathtaking and it's really fun.

Eventually in summer 2025 it became known that the go-kart track in Wuppertal would reopen with a new operator. Yippee!!! Winter is secure again.

In autumn 2025 a whole new opportunity emerged. On the outdoor track in Kerpen you can now ride with organizer 2wheels-company. Unfortunately this is only the very small track where the rental karts usually run and not the big and sprawling Erftland ring. Maybe we'll be allowed to go there sometime if we behave well.

**Kerpen Track Report:**

One lap is relatively short (e.g. compared to Spa). You go left almost the whole time. The grip is excellent. Unfortunately there's nothing you could call a driver area. So it can already get tight when setting up.

The 3 or 4 times I was there, there were only very few participants and it was open pit lane the whole time, so endless driving time!

If there's more going on there in the future, it could get really uncomfortable tight when you set up with bike, accessories and seating.

The organizer rides the whole time himself and is very sympathetic and relaxed.

![Kerpen aerial view](/assets/images/pitbike-09.jpg)

## Chapter XII: A Year is Up, Time to Draw a Conclusion (October 2025)

Like anything new that you have incredibly much fun with, the euphoria isn't the same anymore. Instead of 1-2 times a week, I now go 1-2 times a month. That could be because in winter there are significantly fewer tracks available. In summer there will certainly be a lot of action.

If you like to go fast in circles on a two-wheeler, you should definitely book 2-3 sessions with rental bikes and check it out. As we noticed in our group, not everyone can enjoy this.

For me it was the best discovery in a long time. I get an opportunity here that I've been missing for a long time: Being able to really go for it in winter too :-D

And as the cherry on top, the whole thing is many times cheaper than the race track for the big machine. You pay between €35 and €80 per day with your own bike. Depending on the track and organizer.

**Update February 2026:**

Recently I booked an intensive training session at Wuppertal with Schnurri-Racing. Significantly reduced number of riders and special exercises brought new variety and experience.

I already knew the guys from Schnurri from the beginning when they were instructors at Flybikes24. In the meantime they organize their own events on weekends in Wuppertal. The two (Sascha and Julius) are super sympathetic and very relaxed. At the events you're provided with coffee and water for free. Recently one of the participants even brought homemade muffins for the whole gang. Love it!

The China cracker got a few more improvements. The shock absorber attachment on the IMR/MRF models is very cheaply executed. When you lift the rear you can feel there's a lot of play in it. In other words: wobbles and doesn't fit.

Instead of having a workshop do it with bushings and bells and whistles for a lot of money, I came up with a DIY solution. There are so-called pass screws. I ordered these in the right size (thickness, length) and installed them. The pros might say that's not 100% perfect either. Don't care. My 99% solution is top for me!

![Shock absorber](/assets/images/pitbike-10.jpg)

Since I bought the pass screws in a 10-pack, I would give a suitable set to anyone who just asks nicely.

So-called rivet nuts can also make life much easier. I used these for the fairing so I can screw in the screws without having to put a nut on the back for fastening.

![Swingarm](/assets/images/pitbike-11.jpg)

I also tried a DIY drill starter. Basically it would work. Unfortunately my cordless drill is too weak to work reliably. I've also heard from friends that several of their cordless drills broke with the purchased sets. So I'll stick with kicking or pushing for now.

In a YouTube video by Xpear Pitbike, I saw that intake funnels are always used there. Depending on which RPM range you want the maximum power in, then of different lengths. So I got one of those horns from China. Unfortunately it's made of aluminum and I'm worried that the connection isn't flush and "secondary air" is being drawn. P.S. I really don't know much about carburetors.

So I started CAD again and built a funnel myself and 3D printed it with TPU.

It fits for the most part, but I had to change the jetting quite a bit. Again: I have no idea about it. So everything had to be adjusted through trial and error. I'm just going to claim that after my feeling the power down low has improved a bit.

this will be my story of my pitbike journey. Loaded with ride reports, experiences, tips and tricks, as well as my very personal recommendations.

P.S. it became much much longer than planned.

## Chapter I: The First Date (October 2024)

In our racing crew there had been calls for a while to go pitbiking. For various reasons it just never happened. One has back issues, another is too heavy for the participation requirements and the next one just prefers to drink. But then, in October 2024, three fearless racers set out to Gevelsberg. The crew from German-Pitbike does a great job there. Everything is very sympathetic and well organized. Since we're also among the fastest with our big bikes (NOT!), we decided on the slowest group. Short briefing and test sitting. Feels like fun.

Then we finally go for it. Right in the first turn everything in me screams "WHAT THE HECK IS THIS???". This has about as much in common with the motorcycle riding I know as a V02 slick has with vanilla ice cream.

Well, just keep going. After a few turns my knee touched the ground for 0.02 seconds and I'm totally hooked.

Over the six turns, unfortunately I had four different bikes under me. Each one was different in terms of suspension and brakes. When you're already getting used to a completely new feel, that's not helpful. So on the way back it was already clear that I needed my own bike. The same applied to one of my colleagues, whereas the third one didn't warm up to the whole thing at all.

In defense of German-Pitbike: During the briefing you're told to remember the number of the bike and always take the same one during group changes. Unfortunately after two turns a lady (also a complete beginner) felt overwhelmed in the faster group. Strange, why would that happen?! The organizer then picked me out as the one who would most likely manage to not immediately kill me and others among the non-professionals. As a result, I swapped groups with the worthy lady and now rode different bikes each time.

**Gevelsberg Track Report:** The hall is medium-sized and the track is pretty tight. There are 2-3 places where you roll so slowly in 2nd gear that you feel the engine is about to stall. The asphalt surface has very good grip. There are standing tables and seating areas along the track. The catering area is separated and has everything you need.

## Chapter II: The Aftermath (October 2024)

By then I had read as much as I could about the subject and wasn't willing to spend €2,000 on a new bike (+€400 for necessary upgrades). The goal was to have a bike I could ride in the go-kart halls in the area. I only ruled out a 140 because they don't have oil filters.

The other team member already had his own bike a day after Gevelsberg. The previous owner also introduced my buddy to the world of pitbike WhatsApp groups and I came along for the ride. I wasn't happy with the used market prices, even though my thoughts were naturally only on one thing. All online shops were searched (yes, really ALL) and possibilities of picking up a bargain abroad were explored. Nothing. I'M GOING CRAZY.

Easy man, always breathe through your pants...

To avoid destroying my beautiful, brand-new suit, I first snagged a basic suit on the used market. At least that worked right away.

So first back to riding a rental bike. We found an organizer in Wuppertal where you can ride both with a rental and your own bike. Exactly right for our duo. I had asked the organizer in advance for a IMR/MRF 155 or 160, as that was my favorite for purchase. No problem at all, so off we go.

What can I say, with Daniel from Flybikes24 everything is just right. The bikes are top-notch, the people are top-notch and most importantly: coffee and sweets are included in the price. Here I could ride the same bike all day long and get used to it. This also made it possible to make small chassis optimizations halfway through to adapt them to the gravitational forces of my astral body. Simply awesome.

Unfortunately, unfortunately, unfortunately Daniel doesn't want to part with his used rental bikes. Not even for money. So my procurement crisis continues for the next few days with the soreness of death.

**Wuppertal Track Report:** The hall is pretty small and the track is pretty tight. Compared to Gevelsberg it's even a bit smaller but faster to ride. There also aren't such ultra-slow chicanes like in Gevelsberg, fortunately. The asphalt surface has very good grip. If you set up in the driver's area, unfortunately there aren't enough seating areas due to lack of space, and the dining and toilets are at the other end of the hall. It's just designed for rental karts. The track is unfortunately rather dark in some places due to lack of windows and elaborate lighting.

[Video proof Wuppertal](https://youtu.be/BsYVSYBjZgE)

*Update 01/2026:*

*The situation in Wuppertal has also changed. The lighting is much better. The driver area is tidied up and now really has plenty of space. The new operators are super friendly and always attentive.*

## Chapter III: My Treasure (October 2024)

After a week of agony during which I was certainly just one click away from ordering a new bike online five times, the holy grail appeared in classifieds. An IMR 155 with only 3 operating hours and less than an hour away. The whole thing at a price that at first glance leaves a suspicion of scam. Don't let it bother you, THIS is the opportunity. Appointment to view scheduled for the next evening.

Arriving there, I stand in front of a huge, modern paint shop. No sketchy backyard business or anything like that. The shop's name is the seller's name.

Before I even saw the bike, we chatted for a good half hour. It turned out very early: He's a real racer. Former IDM driver with equipment I can't afford in this lifetime. Several like-new race bikes and campers including trailers with custom paint jobs. Who did that lovely paint for him?? Now for the pitbike. The seller always rides pitbikes on outdoor go-kart tracks in Spain in winter, meets people like Pedro Acosta there, buys the bikes right there and lets them summer there. Funny word. After his bike took a tumble, he just wanted to buy a new bike. Unfortunately, he didn't have the usual 190 in stock, only the 155. Bought it, slapped a turbo kit exhaust on it, and rode.

For him it was clear after one day: Not enough power. So bring it back to Germany and let it sit unused in the garage for four years. Eventually the thing had to go. And here I come. As a serious buyer, I must insist on a short test ride after intensive visual inspection. To be honest: At that price, I would have bought the car completely unseen with advance payment. But okay, starts right up, everything works, pay and load it into the car. By the way: In Spain either everyone is already hard of hearing, or engine sound is gladly tolerated. The thing had an open turbo kit exhaust. No DB killer. When you hit the gas it shatters your eardrums and there's also fire coming out the back. Must have!!!

First learning: If you transport the bike lying down in your car, you have to do more than just lay a blanket under it to protect against leaking fluids. Car stinks. We'll deal with that later. First let me check out my new toy more closely.

## Chapter IV: The First Build (October 2024)

Since I still have to wait for the parts, I start with the fork. Done fork service many times myself. Lecko grease is one quality. Threads are glued with UHU, sliding bearings only fit under the highest tension and the workmanship of all parts is just China. I'm not complaining, I knew what I was getting into. Where possible, I improved the fits with the means I had at my disposal and reassembled everything with fresh oil. What would normally be a job for 2-3 hours cost me certainly 12 hours.

In the meantime the remaining parts have arrived. Everything that just screws on is self-explanatory. I had respect for the valve poppets and valve springs. Eventually it's always the first time. YouTube explains how it works and with a bit of skill you print the appropriate special tool yourself with a 3D printer.

Oh yes, the kick starter teeth from the YX engine are supposed to be much more sturdily built than those from the ZS. But since I don't have an impact driver, I haven't installed them yet.

Now try to install the wheels as centrally as possible. I noticed that my swingarm gets pulled together nicely when tightening the axle nut and is constantly under tension. So not just a little bit, but certainly around 8 mm. So from all sides added some washers (M14 or so) as spacers in between. Works perfectly.

Since the ordered handlebar isn't really flatter than the already installed one, the mounting brackets need to get smaller. So I ordered two different variants directly from China. Hopefully one of them will fit and serve its purpose.

My enthusiasm level at this point is about equal to that of 8-year-old Anton who got a Game Boy for Christmas. I can't contain my hype!!

## Chapter V: The First Ride (November 2024)

I absolutely have to ride and maybe try to get the third person excited about it. Spoiler: He didn't like it again.

Off to Witten with Pitbike-Schule24. It's ridden on a parking lot and in the morning it's unfortunately still wet. Fortunately the sun comes out and from the second turn you can ride properly.

After arriving at the organizer I asked what tire pressure my tires should have. He looks at his phone and says 1.4 bar. Filled and ready to go. Unfortunately after just a few meters I notice that the throttle response isn't okay. Asked the organizer and he had the right solution for me right away. Adjusted the mixture screw and continued.

Everything went well, everything stayed intact and had a lot of fun for the first time with my new favorite toy. I don't even know why everyone complains about the original brake. Everything worked very well. Another spoiler: doesn't apply to indoor go-kart tracks.

[Video proof Witten](https://youtu.be/MOOLd6Idpr0)

**Witten Track Report:** It's a parking lot with good asphalt and some rain gutters. One of them had to be driven through in a lean in the temporarily set up course. Everything went fine. Basically I don't think courses built with tires and tape are ideal. On the parking lot there is an Obelix-sized hinkelstein which had to be avoided in a chicane. If you make a mistake there, bones and bikes only have scrap value. The organizer is okay compared to the others. The guys sometimes come across a bit arrogant. I was disappointed though, because their online presence raised completely different expectations. I had expected to receive driving tips permanently, do small exercises next to the track and basically everything that belongs to a "school". With Flybikes24 there were driving tips. That was gold. Here unfortunately just driving time. I was in Witten a total of two times. The second time a parking lot right next to it was used. It's a bit smaller but has no obstacles like curbs or boulders on it. Anyone who just wants to ride something outdoors in the summer is well served here.
